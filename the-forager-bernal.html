Looking for a Starter Home on the Sunny Side of Bernal Heights

A nurse and a software engineer had a modest wish list — original details, a backyard for a future dog, and a street where they could picture growing old. The asking prices, however, had other plans.

RP
By Rosalind Pacheco
New Bernal Times Real Estate Correspondent · June 18, 2026

Maya Tran and Daniel Osei had been renting a two-bedroom apartment on Precita Avenue for four years when their landlord announced he was selling the building. He was very sorry, he said. The market had been very, very good to him.

Maya, 33, is a labor and delivery nurse at UCSF Medical Center. Daniel, 35, writes software for a climate-tech startup headquartered in the Dogpatch. Together they earn just over $310,000 a year — a figure that, in any other American city, might have them browsing four-bedroom Colonials with a guest suite. In Bernal Heights, it meant they needed to be "realistic," a word that came up constantly in conversation and that they both deployed with the practiced resignation of people who have been to many open houses.

"We weren't asking for much," Maya said, stirring a cortado at the Bernal Bean on Cortland Avenue. "Two bedrooms. One and a half baths if we were lucky. A backyard — even a small one — for the dog we keep promising ourselves. And we wanted to stay in Bernal. We've built a life here."

"We kept telling ourselves: it's just a house. And then we'd walk in and it would smell like someone else's laundry and we'd fall in love anyway."

Their budget was $1.5 million, which they arrived at through a combination of savings, help from Daniel's parents in Accra, and a deep and perhaps delusional optimism. Their agent, a veteran of the Bernal market named Cecelia Ruiz, gently informed them that $1.5 million was, in her words, "a conversation starter."

"Cecelia kept saying 'offers over,'" Daniel recalled. "Every listing. Offers over. I started having dreams where everything in life was 'offers over.' My coffee. My parking spot."

They attended 22 open houses over seven months. They lost three offers — two at asking, one $80,000 over. They cried once, in a car on Andover Street, before pulling themselves together and driving to Tartine for consolation croissants.

Then, in a span of ten days in March, three homes became available within six blocks of their rental. Cecelia called it a "micro-surge." Maya called it "the universe finally getting its act together." They moved quickly.

A wood-framed Edwardian cottage perched above street level on a double set of painted concrete steps — the kind of entrance that makes visitors feel, before they've even turned the knob, that something charming awaits. And mostly, it does. The living room retains its original picture rail molding and a decorative fireplace with a hand-painted tile surround. The kitchen is small but functional, recently refreshed with subway tile and open shelving. The backyard is compact — about 400 square feet of poured concrete and one determined Meyer lemon tree — but it has the southern exposure that makes Bernal feel, on a fog-free afternoon, like an entirely different city.

"I walked in and I said 'this is the one' before I even saw the backyard," Maya said. "Daniel said, 'You say that every time.' I said, 'This time I mean it.'" She had also said it at the last four houses. But something about the afternoon light pooling on the original Douglas fir floors gave this one a different quality of conviction.

The largest of the three, a late Craftsman with a proper front porch and a layout that allows for something the couple had almost stopped believing was possible in their price range: a dedicated home office. Three bedrooms, two full baths, and a renovated kitchen with an actual island — small, but an island. The basement has been finished into a laundry room and bonus storage space, and the backyard has been thoughtfully terraced into two usable levels. The location sits at the lower edge of Bernal, closer to the Mission flats, which means more foot traffic, better taqueria access, and a slightly longer hike up the hill to their favorite park.

Daniel loved it immediately. He stood in the would-be office — a room with good light and a view of the neighbor's fig tree — and said he could already see his standing desk there. Maya appreciated the kitchen but noticed the electrical panel looked original to the Eisenhower era. Their agent Cecelia said, quietly, that the inspector would have "thoughts."

A Victorian rowhouse that wears its age with a certain dignity — arched transom window above the door, original egg-and-dart molding along the ceiling, wide-plank floors that creak in a way that feels earned. At 960 square feet, it is not large; the second bedroom requires a willingness to be creative, or at least friendly with IKEA's space-saving line. But the lot is deep, and the backyard — an unexpectedly generous 40-by-40-foot rectangle — had been recently cleared by the sellers, an optimistic blank canvas of dirt and possibility. The street itself is a gentle one: mostly owner-occupied homes, a neighbor who leaves Meyer lemons on the stoop in season, and the kind of block that holds a raucous Fourth of July party and cleans up meticulously afterward.

The backyard was what got them. "We stood out there and Maya just went quiet," Daniel said. "That was the moment I knew." Maya stood in the center of the patchy dirt rectangle and pictured a raised vegetable bed, a hammock strung between two posts, a medium-sized dog of as-yet-undetermined breed exploring the perimeter. "I didn't need it to be perfect," she said. "I just needed it to be ours."

They Chose Home 3: 25 Elsie Street

The inspection came back clean enough — a few items, nothing structural, nothing that Cecelia hadn't seen a hundred times before. They offered $1,255,000, which was $160,000 over asking and, at the time, felt like an enormous number. The sellers, a retired couple relocating to be near their grandchildren in Portland, accepted within hours.

"We probably could have pushed harder on Shotwell," Daniel admitted, "and gotten more space. But space isn't everything. We kept coming back to the feeling of 25 Elsie. It felt like a home that was waiting to be filled up."

They moved in on a Saturday in early April, carrying boxes up those narrow front steps while their upstairs neighbors — they had upstairs neighbors now, a concept that delighted them — knocked on the door with a bottle of Napa Cab and the information that the block association meets the first Wednesday of every month and they make really excellent tamales.

The kitchen renovation is budgeted for year two. The dog — a rescue greyhound they have already named Mango — arrives in July. The backyard remains, for now, a rectangle of dirt and promise.

"We kept telling ourselves: it's just a house," Maya said, sitting on the back steps in the afternoon sun, a mug of tea balanced on her knee. "And then we moved in, and it just — stopped being a house."

She paused and watched a hummingbird investigate the neighbor's bougainvillea over the fence.

"It's ours," she said.
